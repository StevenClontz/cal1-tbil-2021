Theorem 1.3.4. Limit Properties, I.

Let \(b\text{,}\) \(c\text{,}\) \(L\text{,}\) and \(K\) be real numbers, let \(n\) be a positive integer, and let \(f\) and \(g\) be functions defined on an open interval \(I\) containing \(c\) satisfying

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L \, \text{and} \, \lim_{x \to c} g(x) = K\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then we have the following limits as well.

  1. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to c} b = b\)

  2. \(\displaystyle \lim_{x \to c} x = c\)

  3. Sum/Difference Property \(\lim_{x \to c} (f(x) \pm g(x)) = L \pm K\)

  4. Scalar Multiples Property \(\lim_{x \to c} b \cdot f(x) = bL\)

  5. Product Property \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) \cdot g(x) = LK\)

  6. \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) / g(x) = L/K\) (provided that \(K \ne 0\))

Theorem 1.3.5. Limit Properties, II.

Let \(n\) be a positive integer, and let \(f\) and \(g\) be functions defined on an open interval \(I\) containing \(c\) satisfying

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L \, \text{and} \, \lim_{x \to L} g(x) = K \text{ and } g(L)=K\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then we have the following limits as well.

  1. Power Property \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)^n = L^n\)

  2. Root Property \(\lim_{x \to c} \sqrt[n]{f(x)} = \sqrt[n]{L}\)

  3. Composition Property \(\lim_{x \to c} g(f(x)) = K. \)

Theorem 1.3.8. General Limits of Polynomials.

If \(p(x)\) is a polynomial and \(c\) is a real number, then \(\lim_{x \to c} p(x) = p(c)\text{.}\)

  1. Sums/differences, scalar multiples, and products properties

  3. Powers, sums/differences, scalar multiples, and constants properties

Theorem 1.3.10. General Limits of Rational Functions.

If \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are polynomials, \(c\) is a real number, and \(q(c) \neq 0\) then \(\lim_{x \to c} \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}=\frac{p(c)}{q(c)}\text{.}\)

Activity 1.3.11.

One of the requirements to Theorem 1.3.10 when taking the limit of a rational function \(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) as \(x \to c\) was that we cannot have \(q(c)=0\text{.}\) Is it possible for \(\lim_{x \to c}\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \) to exist if \(q(c)=0\text{?}\)

  1. No, because \(\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) is not defnied at \(x=c\) since \(q(c)=0\text{.}\)

  2. Yes, because if you graph \(f(x)=\frac{x^2-1}{x-1}\text{,}\) the value \(f(1)\) is not defined, but the graph shows that the limit of \(f(x)\) does exist as \(x \to 1\text{.}\)

  3. No, because if you graph \(g(x)=\frac{x^2+1}{x-1}\text{,}\) the value \(g(1)\) is not defined and the graph shows that the limit of \(\lim_{x \to c}g(x)\) does not exist.

  4. Yes, because Theorem 1.3.10 does not say anything about the existence of \(\lim_{x \to c}\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\) when \(q(c)=0\text{.}\)

Activity 1.3.13.

In activity Activity 1.2.12 you studied the velocity of Usain Bolt in his Beijing 100 meters dash. We will now study this situation analytically. To make our computations simpler, we will approximate that he could run 100 meters in 10 seconds and we will consider the model \(d=f(t)=t^2\text{,}\) where \(d\) is the distance in meters and \(t\) is the time in seconds.

The average velocity is the ratio distance covered over time elapsed. If we consider the interval that starts at \(t=a\) and has width \(h\text{,}\) written \([a,a+h]\text{,}\) the average velocity on this interval is \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{(a+h) - a} = \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\text{.}\) The instantaneous velocity at time \(t=a\) is given by:

\begin{equation*} \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \end{equation*}
(a)

Compute the average velocity on the interval \([5,6]\text{.}\) We think of this interval as \([5,5+h]\) for the value of \(h=1\text{.}\)

(b)

Compute the average velocity starting at 5 seconds, but now with \(h=0.5\) seconds.

(c)

We want to study the instantaneous velocity at \(a=5\) seconds. Find an expression for the average velocity on the interval \([5,5+h]\text{,}\) where \(h\) is an unspecified value.

(d)

Expand your expression. When \(h \neq 0\text{,}\) you can simplify it!

(e)

Recall that the instantaneous velocity is the limit of your expression as \(h\to 0\text{.}\) Find the instantaneous velocity given by this model at \(t=5\) seconds.

(f)

The model \(d=f(t)=t^2\) does not really capture the real-world situation. Think of at least one reason why this model does not fit the scenario of Usain Bolt's 100 meters dash.
