Section 2.1 Derivatives graphically and numerically (DF1)

Learning Outcomes

  • Estimate the value of a derivative using difference quotients, and draw corresponding secant and tangent lines on the graph of a function.

Activity 2.1.1.

In this activity you will study the velocity of a ball falling under gravity. The height of the ball is given by the formula \(f(t) = 64-16(t-1)^2\text{.}\) For your convenience, below you will find a table of values for \(f(t)\text{.}\) Recall that the average velocity is given by the change in height over the change in time.

(a)

What was the average velocity on the interval \([1,2]\text{?}\) What about on the interval \([2,3]\text{?}\)

(b)

What was the average velocity on the interval \([1.5,2]\text{?}\) What about on the interval \([2,2.5]\text{?}\)

(c)

What was the average velocity on the interval \([1.75,2]\text{?}\) What about on the interval \([2,2.25]\text{?}\)

(d)

If we wanted to approximate the velocity at the instant \(t=2\text{,}\) what would be your best estimate for this instantaneous velocity?

Observation 2.1.3.

If we want to study the velocity at the instant \(t=2\text{,}\) it is helpful to study the average velocity on small intervals around \(t=2\text{.}\) If we consider the interval \([2,2+h]\text{,}\) where \(h\) is the width of the interval, the average velocity is given by the difference quotient

\begin{equation*} \frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{(2+h)-2} = \frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h}. \end{equation*}
Activity 2.1.4.

Consider the height of the ball falling under gravity as in Table 2.1.2 .

(a)

What was the average velocity on the interval \([2,2+h]\) for \(h=1\) and \(h=-1\text{?}\)

(b)

What was the average velocity on the interval \([2,2+h]\) for \(h=0.5\) and \(h=-0.5\text{?}\)

(c)

What was the average velocity on the interval \([2,2+h]\) for \(h=0.25\) and \(h=-0.25\text{?}\)

(d)

How would you express the instaneous velocity at \(t=2\) the average velocity and the language of limits?

Definition 2.1.5.

The instanteous velocity at \(t=a\) is the limit as \(h \to 0\) of the difference quotient \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\text{.}\) In the activity above the instantaneous velocity at \(t=2\) is given by the limit

\begin{equation*} v(2) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(2+h)-f(2)}{h} \end{equation*}
Observation 2.1.6.

The slope of the secant line to \(f(x)\) through the points \(x=a\) and \(x=b\) is given by the difference quotient

\begin{equation*} \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}. \end{equation*}
Activity 2.1.7.

In this activity you will study the slope of a graph at a point. The graph of the function \(g(x)\) is given below. For your convenience, below you will find a table of values for \(g(x)\text{.}\) Recall that the slope of a line through the points \((a,g(a))\) and \((b,g(b))\) is given by \(m = \frac{g(b)-g(a)}{b-a}\)

(a)

What is the slope of the line through \((2,g(2))\) and \((3,g(3))\text{?}\) What about through \((3,g(3))\) and \((4,g(4))\text{?}\) Draw these lines on the graph of \(g(x)\text{.}\)

(b)

What is the slope of the line through \((2.5,g(2.5))\) and \((3,g(3))\text{?}\) What about through \((3,g(3))\) and \((3.5,g(3.5))\text{?}\) Draw these lines on the graph of \(g(x)\text{.}\)

(c)

Draw the line tangent to \(g(x)\) at \(x=3\text{.}\) What would be your best estimate for the slope of this tangent line?

Observation 2.1.10.

The slope of the secant line to \(f(x)\) through the points \(x=a\) and \(x=b\) is given by the difference quotient \(\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\) and as \(b\) gets closer to \(a\text{,}\) the slope of the secant line tends to the slope of the tangent line. Letting \(b=a+h\text{,}\) we say that the slope of the tangent line at \(x=a\) is given by the limit

\begin{equation*} \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}. \end{equation*}
Definition 2.1.11.

The derivative of \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\text{,}\) denoted \(f'(a)\text{,}\) is given by

\begin{equation*} f'(a) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}. \end{equation*}
Activity 2.1.12.

Suppose that the function \(f(x)\) gives the position of an object at time \(x\text{.}\) Which of the following quantities are the same? Select all!

  1. The value of the derivative of \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\)

  2. The slope of the tangent line to \(f(x)\) at \(x=a\)

  3. The instantaneous velocity of the object at \(x=a\)

  4. The difference quotient \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \)

  5. The limit \(\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \)

Observation 2.1.13.

We can use the difference quotient \(\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \) for small values of \(h\) to estimate \(f'(a)\text{,}\) the value of the derivative at \(x=a\text{.}\)

Activity 2.1.14.

Suppose that you know that the function \(g(x)\) has values \(g(-0.5)=7\text{,}\) \(g(0)=4\text{,}\) and \(g(0.5)=2\text{.}\) What is your best estimate for \(g'(0)?\)

  1. \(\displaystyle g'(0) \approx -3\)

  2. \(\displaystyle g'(0) \approx -2\)

  3. \(\displaystyle g'(0) \approx -6\)

  4. \(\displaystyle g'(0) \approx -4\)

  5. \(\displaystyle g'(0) \approx -5\)

Activity 2.1.15.

Suppose that you know that the function \(f(x)\) has value \(f(1)=3\) and has derivative at \(x=1\) given by \(f'(1)=2\text{.}\) Which of the following scenarios is most likely?

  1. \(f(2)=3\) because the function is constant

  2. \(f(2)=2\) because the derivative is constant

  3. \(f(2) \approx 1\) because the function's output decreases by about 2 units for each increase by 1 unit in the input

  4. \(f(2) \approx 5\) because the function's output increases by about 2 units for each increase by 1 unit in the input

Observation 2.1.16.

We can use the derivative at \(x=a\) to estimate the increase/decrease of the function \(f(x)\) close to \(x=a\text{.}\)
